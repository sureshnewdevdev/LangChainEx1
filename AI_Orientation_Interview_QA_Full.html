What is AI?

Q1. What is Artificial Intelligence?

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is the ability of machines or software to perform tasks that normally require human intelligence, such as understanding language, learning from experience, reasoning, and making decisions.

Examples: Chatbots, recommendation engines (Netflix, Amazon), self‑driving cars, fraud detection, face recognition.

Q2. What are the main subfields of AI and what do they focus on?
  • Machine Learning (ML): Algorithms learn patterns from data to make predictions.
  • Deep Learning (DL): Uses multi-layer neural networks for complex tasks like vision and speech.
  • Natural Language Processing (NLP): Understanding and generating human language.
  • Computer Vision: Interpreting images and videos.
  • Robotics: Physical robots that sense and act in the real world.
  • Expert Systems: Rule-based systems that mimic human experts.

AI vs ML vs DL

Q3. Explain the difference between AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning with examples.

AI: The overall concept of machines showing intelligence. Example: A chess-playing program that follows fixed rules.

Machine Learning: A subset of AI where models learn from data instead of being explicitly programmed. Example: Email spam filter trained on labeled emails.

Deep Learning: A subset of ML using deep neural networks (many layers). Example: Image classification using CNNs or voice assistants using RNN/Transformer networks.

Hierarchy: Deep Learning ⊂ Machine Learning ⊂ Artificial Intelligence

Q4. When would you choose traditional ML over Deep Learning?

Use traditional ML when:

  • The dataset is relatively small.
  • Features are well-understood and can be engineered manually.
  • You need easy interpretability (e.g., decision trees, linear models).
  • Infrastructure is limited and you cannot train large neural networks.

Evolution of AI

Q5. Briefly describe the evolution of AI from rule-based systems to LLMs.
  1. 1950s–1980s: Symbolic AI / Expert Systems – Manually coded rules (IF–THEN). Hard to scale and brittle.
  2. 1990s–2010: Classical Machine Learning – Algorithms like SVM, Random Forests, logistic regression; driven by feature engineering.
  3. 2012–2018: Deep Learning Era – Neural networks for images, speech, translation (AlexNet, CNNs, RNNs).
  4. 2018–Present: Transformer & LLM Era – Self‑attention models, massive pretraining, generative text & code (GPT, BERT, Claude, Gemini).
Q6. What made Transformers a turning point in AI history?

The Transformer architecture introduced self‑attention, which allows models to focus on different parts of the input sequence in parallel. This removed the sequential bottleneck of RNNs and enabled training on huge datasets using GPUs/TPUs, leading directly to today’s LLMs.

Intro to Generative AI

Q7. What is Generative AI and how is it different from traditional predictive models?

Generative AI creates new content such as text, images, audio, video, or code. Traditional models typically only predict a label or value.

Example:

  • Predictive model: Predict if a transaction is fraud (Yes/No).
  • Generative model: Generate a full customer email explaining a fraud alert in natural language.
Q8. Give some real-world applications of Generative AI.
  • Chatbots and virtual assistants
  • Code generation and debugging
  • Marketing content and ad copy creation
  • Image generation for design and art
  • Summarization of long documents
  • Personalized learning materials

Large Language Models (LLMs)

Q9. What is a Large Language Model and how is it trained?

A Large Language Model (LLM) is a neural network (usually Transformer-based) trained on huge corpora of text to predict the next token in a sequence.

Training process:

  1. Collect large text datasets (web pages, books, code, etc.).
  2. Tokenize text into subword units.
  3. Train the model to minimize next-token prediction loss.
  4. Optionally fine-tune on specific tasks or with human feedback.
Q10. What are some limitations of LLMs?
  • They can hallucinate – confidently produce incorrect information.
  • No real-time knowledge unless connected to external tools.
  • Potentially biased outputs depending on training data.
  • High compute and memory requirements.
  • May leak sensitive training data if not properly aligned.

Ethical AI and Responsible Development

Q11. What are key principles of Ethical AI?
  • Fairness: Avoid discrimination and bias.
  • Accountability: Clear ownership for outcomes.
  • Transparency: Explain how models make decisions.
  • Privacy: Protect user data and follow regulations.
  • Safety & Security: Prevent misuse, attacks, and harmful outputs.
  • Human Control: Humans remain in control of critical decisions.
Q12. Give an example of bias in an AI system and how you would mitigate it.

Example: A hiring model that ranks resumes may favor one gender because the historical dataset contained more successful male candidates.

Mitigation steps:

  • Audit data for imbalance and remove sensitive attributes.
  • Apply debiasing techniques (reweighing, adversarial fairness).
  • Continuously monitor outcomes by demographic group.
  • Include human review for high-impact decisions.

Claude 3 Family

Q13. What is Claude 3 and where does it fit in the LLM ecosystem?

Claude 3 is a family of models (Haiku, Sonnet, Opus) developed by Anthropic. It focuses on safety, long-context reasoning, and business use cases such as document analysis, coding, and knowledge assistants.

Q14. How would you choose between Claude, GPT, and other LLMs for a project?

Consider:

  • Task type: coding, chat, summarization, multi-modal needs.
  • Context length: very long documents may need larger context (e.g., Claude or Gemini).
  • Safety & compliance: some models have stronger guardrails.
  • Cost & latency: price per 1K tokens, response speed.
  • Enterprise features: data retention policies, SSO, logging.

GPT-Series

Q15. What does “GPT” stand for and what are its key characteristics?

GPT stands for Generative Pretrained Transformer.

  • Uses Transformer architecture with self-attention.
  • Pretrained on large-scale text, then optionally fine-tuned.
  • Can perform many tasks via prompting (zero-shot / few-shot).
  • Supports tools like function calling, browsing, and code execution, depending on the platform.
Q16. How does GPT handle multiple tasks without task-specific training?

GPT is trained on diverse datasets that contain examples of many tasks. During inference, instructions in the prompt guide the model on what to do. This is called in-context learning: the model learns from examples provided in the prompt instead of retraining the weights.

Gemini Models

Q17. What is unique about Google’s Gemini models?

Gemini models are natively multimodal, meaning they can process text, images, audio, and video in a single model. They offer very large context windows and are deeply integrated with Google products (Workspace, Search, Android, etc.).

Q18. In what scenarios would you prefer Gemini over other LLMs?
  • When you need tight integration with Google ecosystem (Docs, Sheets, Drive).
  • When tasks combine images + text (e.g., analyzing a chart plus instructions).
  • When extremely long context is needed for videos or large documents.

Open-source alternatives (Mistral, Llama, etc.)

Q19. Why do companies use open-source LLMs instead of only proprietary ones?
  • Cost control: Run models on their own infrastructure.
  • Customization: Fine-tune with private data.
  • Data residency: Keep all data on-premise or in a specific region.
  • Flexibility: No dependency on a single vendor.
  • Offline usage: Useful for edge or air‑gapped environments.
Q20. Name a few popular open-source models and typical use cases.
  • Llama 3: General-purpose chat and coding.
  • Mistral / Mixtral: Efficient models for chat, tools, and RAG.
  • Gemma: Lightweight models for research and fine-tuning.
  • Phi-3: Small models optimized for reasoning on limited hardware.

Ollama

Q21. What is Ollama and how is it used in a local development workflow?

Ollama is a tool that makes it easy to download and run LLMs locally on your machine. Developers can call these models via simple HTTP APIs from Python, JS, or any language.

Example commands:

ollama run llama3
ollama run mistral
Q22. What are the advantages of running LLMs with Ollama on your laptop?
  • No data leaves your machine – good for privacy.
  • Zero per‑token cloud cost after model download.
  • Useful for offline demos and experiments.
  • Easy integration with local RAG using tools like LangChain and LlamaIndex.

Use cases for LLM

Q23. List some strong business use cases for LLMs in enterprises.
  • Knowledge assistants over internal documents (RAG portals).
  • Customer support chatbots and ticket triage.
  • Automated email and report drafting.
  • Code generation, code review, and test-case creation.
  • Contract review and clause extraction in legal teams.
  • Summarizing meeting transcripts and action item extraction.
Q24. How would you explain RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) to a non-technical stakeholder?

RAG combines a language model with a private knowledge base. Instead of the model guessing from memory, it first searches your documents, retrieves the most relevant passages, and then uses those passages to generate an answer.

In simple terms: Google-like search + ChatGPT-style answer on your own company data.

LLM Best Practices

Q25. What are some best practices for building LLM applications?
  • Use structured prompts and system instructions.
  • Prefer RAG over fine-tuning as a first step.
  • Implement guardrails for safety and content filtering.
  • Log prompts and outputs for monitoring and debugging.
  • Evaluate outputs with both automatic metrics and human review.
  • Version prompts, retrieval logic, and models.
Q26. When would you fine-tune a model instead of only using RAG?

Fine-tuning is useful when:

  • You need a specific tone or style consistently (brand voice).
  • Tasks are very structured, like form-filling or classification.
  • Latency is critical and you want a smaller specialized model.
  • You have a large set of high-quality labeled examples.

Security considerations

Q27. What are common security risks in LLM applications?
  • Prompt injection: User or document content tries to override system instructions.
  • Data leakage: Sensitive information accidentally returned in responses.
  • Model abuse: Generating malicious content (phishing, malware).
  • Supply-chain risks: Using unverified open-source models or libraries.
  • Unauthorized access: Lack of authentication around LLM endpoints.
Q28. How can you protect sensitive data when using third-party LLM APIs?
  • Use vendors that offer no-training-on-your-data guarantees.
  • Mask or tokenize sensitive fields before sending to the API.
  • Apply strict access control and logging on who can call the model.
  • Use private or dedicated deployments where possible.
  • Regularly review compliance (GDPR, HIPAA, etc.).
